Six years into working in tech, I still Google things I feel like I should have memorized by now. For a long time that felt like a character flaw. It isn't.

## The lie imposter syndrome tells you

It says everyone else has it figured out and you're the only one winging it. This is empirically false. Every senior engineer I've worked with Googles syntax. Everyone asks "stupid" questions in Slack. The difference is they've stopped feeling bad about it.

## What actually changes with experience

You don't stop not knowing things. You just get better at:

- Knowing what you don't know
- Knowing where to look
- Knowing when something is genuinely hard vs. just unfamiliar

That's it. The uncertainty doesn't go away. You just stop treating it as evidence of incompetence.

## The thing that actually helped me

Writing things down. When I explained something to myself in plain text, I'd quickly find the holes in my understanding. Teaching is the best test of whether you actually know something.

It also meant that the next time I hit the same problem, I had my own notes to reference — not Stack Overflow, not a doc, just past-me explaining it to future-me.

## What I tell junior engineers

You're not behind. You're just new. The goal isn't to eliminate uncertainty — it's to get comfortable operating inside it.
